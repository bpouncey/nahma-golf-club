# Nahma Golf Club — Layout References

**Version:** 1.0 (May 2026)  
**Purpose:** Curated reference sites for Claude Design. Each entry specifies exactly what to borrow and what to ignore. These are not mood board vibes — they are precise instructions.

---

## How to Use This Document

Each reference is broken into three sections:
- **What it does well** — the specific patterns to emulate
- **What to take** — exact elements to borrow
- **What to ignore** — what would break the Nahma brand if copied

---

## Reference 1: Cabot Cape Breton (cabotlinks.com)

**Why it's relevant:** The best example of photography-led storytelling in golf web design. Big landscape imagery, confident typography on top of photos, no clutter.

**What it does well:**
- Full-bleed hero photography that lets the course speak for itself
- Very clean overlay text — no over-designed effects, just type on photo
- Section transitions that feel like turning pages, not a tech product scrolling through slides
- Consistent use of one large hero photo + supporting smaller photos (not gallery grids)

**What to take:**
- Hero section structure: full viewport height, single dominant photo, minimal text overlay, two CTAs
- Photo-to-text section rhythm: don't over-describe the course, let the photos carry and text supports
- White space discipline: they never cram. Breathing room between sections signals quality.
- Section heading scale: they use large, condensed headings — similar intent to Barlow Condensed

**What to ignore:**
- The specific color palette (theirs skews to cool blues/grays — ours is warm green/cream/terracotta)
- The ultra-minimal navigation style (theirs is very sparse — Nahma should still have a visible nav)
- Any luxury resort pricing/booking patterns (Nahma is public access, $18 green fees — don't signal $400/night vibes)

## Reference 2: Bandon Dunes (bandondunesgolf.com)

**Why it's relevant:** Shows how a destination golf course signals "worth the drive" without being pretentious. Shares the same core challenge as Nahma: convincing someone to travel somewhere remote for the experience.

**What it does well:**
- The hero immediately sells the landscape, not the amenities
- Stat/feature strips between photo sections (like their "Miles of Shoreline" type callouts)
- Section structure that builds a case: setting → the courses → rates → groups → food/drink
- Navigation that converts: clear CTAs visible at scroll, never buried

**What to take:**
- Stat strip pattern directly after the hero (matches our Trust Bar concept exactly)
- "Setting first, specs second" page architecture — mirror this for the homepage section order
- The way they handle the Group Outings section: dark section, big headline, single CTA, phone number beneath
- Foot of page CTA section above footer: "Ready to play?" type closing section

**What to ignore:**
- Multi-course complexity (they have 5 courses — Nahma has 1, so do not borrow multi-level nav or course selector UI)
- The resort/lodging UX patterns (rooms, packages, accommodations — none of that applies)
- Overly cinematic video backgrounds (Nahma has no video asset and shouldn't simulate having one)

## Reference 3: Pinehurst Resort (pinehurst.com)

**Why it's relevant:** Best example of heritage typography in golf. Shows how to make a course feel historic without feeling dusty. Enclosed compositions, serif confidence, structured layouts.

**What it does well:**
- Section headers with flanking rule lines (matches our overline pattern exactly)
- Typography as a structural element — headings do the visual work, not boxes or cards
- Historical identity handled with precision: the year is there, but it doesn't shout
- "Course as character" writing — they write about the course like it has personality, not like a spec sheet

**What to take:**
- The overline + heading + rule combination in their section entries — this is our exact typographic pattern
- How they handle rates: clean, structured, not buried in a table with too many columns
- The footer structure: clear NAP information, season note, minimal nav links
- Section background alternation rhythm: light → dark → light → dark keeps visual interest without needing complex components

**What to ignore:**
- Serif typefaces (Pinehurst uses traditional serifs; Nahma uses Barlow Condensed + DM Sans — do not copy their font choices)
- The country club exclusivity feel (Pinehurst reads as members-first; Nahma is walk-in public)
- Any booking/reservation UI patterns (Nahma explicitly does not require tee times)

## Reference 4: Malbon Golf (malbongolf.com)

**Why it's relevant:** The best current example of modern-vintage brand execution in golf. Shows how to do heritage without being old-fashioned. Also the most visually similar color sensibility to Nahma's terracotta/cream/green palette.

**What it does well:**
- Mid-century collegiate geometry executed cleanly — enclosed compositions, tight containment
- Overline + heading typographic motif (very close to our overline pattern)
- Product/feature cards with overline labels in a secondary accent color
- The way terracotta/rust tones work as accents without overwhelming the palette

**What to take:**
- Card structure: overline label in accent color, condensed heading, short body text — this is the exact component pattern for our league and outing cards
- The "enclosed composition" feel: headlines feel like they're inside a frame, not floating
- Color restraint: accent color appears 1–2 times per section maximum — confirms our own rule
- The section-to-section visual rhythm: they don't oversell every transition

**What to ignore:**
- Streetwear/apparel UI patterns (product grids, add-to-cart, size selectors — none of this applies)
- The heavy social proof approach (they lean heavily on Instagram/culture; Nahma's social proof is Google reviews and 100 years of history)
- Any cart/checkout or e-commerce patterns

## Reference 5: Eastside Golf (eastsidegolf.com)

**Why it's relevant:** Shows how a golf brand with strong identity can feel premium without luxury price signaling. Good reference for how to handle the "accessible but quality" tension Nahma has.

**What it does well:**
- Hero text that leads with identity, not inventory ("WHERE DO YOU PLAY?" not "Book a tee time")
- Community/culture sections that feel genuine rather than corporate
- The leagues section structure: clear, card-based, no over-designed UI
- Handles dark and light sections cleanly without design feeling inconsistent

**What to take:**
- League card structure: day of week as overline label, league name as heading, simple body description — mirrors our Section 7 leagues layout exactly
- How they handle the community narrative section: two-column text + photo, no over-engineering
- The culture/identity voice showing through the layout — not just through copy, but through what gets visual emphasis

**What to ignore:**
- The apparel/brand extension UI (Eastside is heavily a clothing brand; Nahma sells rounds of golf)
- Overly casual/streetwear layout aesthetics (their grid breaks are loose; Nahma's should be structured)
- Anything that signals youth/urban demographics (different audience entirely)

## Reference 6: Mid-Century Country Club Programs (printed ephemera)

**Why it's relevant:** The design system's "Modern Vintage Country Club" concept is rooted in mid-century club programs and scorecards — 1960s–70s golf club aesthetics. These aren't websites; they're the compositional DNA behind our brand.

**What to emulate (visual language, not literal copies):**
- **Enclosed compositions:** Section headers that feel "framed" — the overline + flanking rules pattern is directly from this
- **Condensed uppercase typography stacked tightly:** Barlow Condensed Bold at display scale is the digital equivalent of the classic club program headline
- **Limited color discipline:** Club programs were typically 2-color or 3-color printing. Our palette restriction (green, terracotta, cream) mirrors that constraint
- **Structural borders and rules:** Thin decorative rules used for section breaks, label containers, and information hierarchy — not gradients or drop shadows
- **Numbers as design elements:** Stat sections where "1922" or "PAR 36" are treated like display type, not data points

**What this means practically:**
- When Claude Design generates section headers, they should feel like they could be printed on a 1967 club program
- When in doubt on whether an element "fits," ask: would this look at home on a 1960s country club scorecard or tournament program?
- Reject anything that looks like it was generated by a modern SaaS template

## What None of These References Have (and Nahma Should)

- **The bay.** No reference site has a lake literally adjacent to the fairway. Lean into this harder than any reference. It is the single differentiating visual fact.
- **The price.** $18 is unusually accessible. Don't hide it. Reference sites often obscure pricing. Nahma should surface it early.
- **The walk-in ethos.** Most reference sites imply reservations required. Nahma's "show up and play" is a selling point — make it visible.
