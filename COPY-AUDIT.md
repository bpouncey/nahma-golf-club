# Copy Audit — Nahma Golf Club
*Lens: world-class copywriter · May 2026*

---

## The Big Picture

The site's voice is genuinely good — sparse, declarative, unhurried. That's rare for a golf course website and worth protecting. The threat isn't bad writing; it's **repetition eroding the signal**. A few numbers (9 holes, par 36, 2,875 yards, $18, 1922) appear so relentlessly that they lose meaning before the reader is halfway through the site. There are also a handful of phrases that read as AI-generated, which undercut the authentic tone of everything around them.

### The Stat Carousel Problem
These five facts appear across nearly every page, sometimes multiple times on the *same* page:
- 9 holes · Par 36 · 2,875 yards · Est. 1922 · Green fees from $18

The homepage alone cycles through them three times: trust bar → stats grid → closing CTA. The-course page surfaces them in breadcrumbs, body copy, and "By the Numbers." They're useful context, but right now they function like a loop that readers start skimming past.

**Rule of thumb:** These facts earn one prominent placement per page. Everything after that should either add context or get cut.

---

## Per-Page Findings

---

### Homepage (`/`)

**What's working:** The hero line is excellent. "Some golf courses have a view. Nahma Golf Club sits on the shore." is the best sentence on the site. The clubhouse paragraph ("It has a way of turning into an afternoon") and the outings list with real named events (The Geno Open, LaLonde Family Scramble) are both strong.

**Issues:**

The stats grid section — "Nine Holes. One Unforgettable Setting." — restates 9 holes, par 36, 2,875 yards, 1922, and public access. All five just appeared in the trust bar fifteen seconds ago. The grid itself is fine; the label text under each stat repeats facts the reader just absorbed.

The closing CTA ("No tee time needed. Walk-ins welcome. Open May through September.") restates the hero subhead nearly verbatim ("Nine holes on the shore of Big Bay de Noc. Public course. Walk-ins welcome."). One of these is doing the same job.

**Specific flag:** "Memberships start at $75. Individual season membership from $450." leads with the two most different-looking prices ($75 vs $450) without explaining that $75 is a student rate. A reader who doesn't know this sees a suspicious price spread and clicks away.

---

### About (`/about/`)

**What's working:** The mission paragraph is honest and specific. "We believe golf should be available to anyone who wants to play it" works because it's followed by evidence (pricing, leagues, no membership requirement) rather than left as a slogan.

**Issues:**

The three-card structure — **What We Are / What We're Not / What This Means for You** — is AI-framework copy. The headers are mechanical and the third card ("What This Means for You: Show Up. Play. $18 gets you on the course. No other barriers.") is particularly thin. "No other barriers" is a weak closer and the whole card basically restates "What We Are."

The facts bar (1922, 9, 36, 2,875, $18) appears immediately below a breadcrumb block that already lists "9 Holes · Par 36 · Public Course · Walk-Ins Welcome." Same facts, twice, in adjacent sections.

**Recommendation:** Cut the three-card structure or rewrite the headers so they carry their own meaning. "What We're Not" is actually the most interesting card because it's specific — lean into that.

---

### The Course (`/the-course/`)

**What's working:** The greens description ("They reward precision over power. If you play here once, you will want to know them better before you come back.") is the best descriptive writing on the site. The triple negative — "No driving range. No dress code. No tee time required." — is clean and confident.

**Issues:**

The 2,875 yards / par 36 / 9 holes / 1922 cluster appears in: (1) the hero breadcrumb ("Nine holes on the shore of Big Bay de Noc. Par 36, 2,875 yards."), (2) the layout section body copy ("plays 2,875 yards from the tips, par 36"), and (3) the "By the Numbers" widget. Three times on one page. The body copy reference is the most earned; the other two are fighting for the same space.

The CTA at the bottom ("Nine holes start at $18. Eighteen holes (replaying the course) are $28.") is fine, but "replaying the course" in parentheses has been explained two other places on this page already.

---

### Clubhouse (`/clubhouse/`)

**What's working:** The opening paragraph is strong — "After 9 holes on the shore of Big Bay de Noc, the round isn't over. It's just moving inside." The 19th hole framing works.

**Issues — AI slop:**

The Snacks card bullets are the most obviously AI-generated text on the site:
> "Chips and sides / Packaged snacks / Quick grab items"

"Quick grab items" is a warehouse-inventory phrase, not a bar menu description. "Packaged snacks" is equally lifeless. These bullets undercut the whole page's tone. If all it is is chips and snacks, just say that.

The stat callout at the bottom (9 Holes on the Bay / 1922 Established / $18 Green Fees From) serves no function here — it's not a page where the reader needs those numbers. They're not shopping for a course on this page; they're already reading about the bar.

**Issues — redundancy:**

"A round at Nahma Golf Club doesn't end when you sink the final putt on hole 9." restates the hero subhead ("After 9 holes on the shore of Big Bay de Noc, the round isn't over"). Both are in consecutive sections on the same scroll.

"The clubhouse has been part of the Nahma Golf Club experience since 1922" — the 1922 reference feels shoehorned here. A bar being old isn't a selling point for a beer.

---

### Outings (`/outings/`)

**What's working:** The named past outings (Manistique Paper Outing, Geno Open, LaLonde Family Scramble, etc.) are the single best proof element on the site. Keep every one of them. The "Three Steps to Book" section is appropriately simple.

**Issues — AI slop:**

> "Most outing coordinators underestimate how much the post-round experience matters."

"Outing coordinators" is marketing-speak that doesn't match the voice of this site. Real people planning a company scramble don't think of themselves as "outing coordinators." Cut the sentence; the paragraph below it makes the point without preamble.

**Issues — structural:**

"Michigan Golf Trips" as an outing type card (card 05) doesn't belong in the same list as Corporate Outings, Fundraisers, and Family Reunions. Those are all *who books the outing*; "Michigan Golf Trips" is a *context for why someone is in the area*. It reads as a keyword being wedged into a functional list. The content itself is fine — move it to the intro section or the Escanaba/Manistique SEO pages where it fits naturally.

"Annual Scrambles" card closes with: "Private bookings available throughout the season." Generic filler. Every card on this page is already in the context of private bookings.

---

### Leagues (`/leagues/`)

**What's working:** "You show up on league night, you are in." is the best CTA line on the page. The voice throughout is consistent and unforced.

**Issues:**

The "How to Join" Step 2 lists all three league nights by name and day again — Men's Tuesday, Scramble Wednesday, Women's Thursday — right after the three cards above already listed them. The step only needs to say "arrive by 6 PM on your chosen night."

"One of the best midweek things to do in the area." (in the Wednesday All Scramble card description) — this is a light SEO phrase and it shows. The card is already making the case through specifics; this line adds nothing.

---

### History (`/history/`)

**What's working:** "Nahma Golf Club has never gone private. It has never become a resort operation." — this is a strong statement and it lands.

**Issues — repetition:**

The 1922 / over a century drum gets beaten six times on a single page:
1. "established in 1922" (founding section body)
2. "More than a century later" (founding section)
3. "That original intention has held for over a hundred years" (founding section)
4. "100+ Years of Play" (facts bar)
5. "over a century" (legacy card)
6. "It has remained what it was built to be" (founding section close)

By the third mention, the reader has stopped registering it. Pick two — one in the founding section, one at the end as a callback — and let the others go.

**Issues — AI slop:**

> "The flat terrain along Big Bay de Noc was part of the site selection."

"Site selection" is a corporate planning term, not historical narrative. Nobody in 1922 called it that. "The founders chose this site deliberately" (two sentences earlier) makes the same point in language that fits the page.

> "That continuity is not accidental. It is the result of a course that was built with the community in mind from the beginning and has stayed true to that original purpose."

This pair of sentences is the most formulaic writing on the page. "Not accidental" is a weak setup, and "stayed true to that original purpose" is a cliché. The paragraph that follows — about leagues, scrambles, outings, generation after generation — makes the point without needing this throat-clearing.

---

### Rates (`/rates/`)

**What's working:** The table format is clear and the contextual notes ("UPGA membership is separate from a Nahma Golf Club membership") prevent confusion.

**Issues:**

> "A Nahma Golf Club membership pays for itself quickly."
> [later] "At $18 per 9-hole round, a single membership pays for itself after 25 rounds. Play more and you're saving on every visit."

"Pays for itself" appears twice, once vague and once specific. Keep the specific version; cut the setup sentence.

"Play more and you're saving on every visit." — tautological. If you play more rounds than the breakeven, you save. The math already said this. Cut the sentence.

The "No Carry-Ons" section:
> "Personal carts may not be brought on the course without the annual trail fee. No carry-on carts allowed under any circumstances."

"No carry-on carts" is repeated within three lines. The second sentence is stricter than the first (the trail fee is the mechanism, not a blanket ban) — clarify and consolidate to one sentence.

---

### Rentals (`/rentals/`)

**What's working:** Lean, functional page. The payment and walking notes add real value for visitors who might have questions.

**Issues:**

> "We accept credit cards, debit cards, and NFC mobile payments. No cash required, though cash is welcome too."

"No cash required, though cash is welcome too" contradicts itself in the same breath. Pick a framing: either lead with "Cards and mobile pay accepted" (which implies cash works too) or say "Cash, card, and mobile pay accepted." The hedging reads uncertain.

The trail fee and storage section (own cart) is fine but the `May–Sep / Season Open` callout at the bottom adds nothing that isn't already said elsewhere on the page.

---

### Tee Times (`/tee-times/`)

**What's working:** The evening round tip — "arriving by 7 PM gives you comfortable time to finish nine holes before dark" — is specific and useful. This is the right kind of practical detail.

**Issues:**

The intro paragraph ends: "the philosophy has always been the same: get people on the course, no barriers." Then immediately:

> "No Reservation Needed / Walk-Ins Welcome / No online booking, no phone-ahead required for individuals and small groups."

That's the same thing twice in a row. The callout block just said what the paragraph said.

The 1922 reference ("Established in 1922 on the shore of Big Bay de Noc, Nahma is one of the oldest public courses in Delta County.") — this is an SEO page for a transactional search ("tee times nahma" or "no tee time required golf UP"). The reader is not here for history; they're here to confirm they can show up without a reservation. The 1922 line can go.

---

### Contact (`/contact/`)

Clean, functional. No significant issues — this page is doing exactly what it should.

---

### SEO Pages (`/golf-near-*/`)

**General assessment:** The structural repetition across these five pages is intentional for SEO and fine. The best pages are the ones with a specific local insight that earns the reader's trust. The worst sections are the templated course-detail cards, which become invisible on the second page a reader visits.

**What's working by page:**
- **Escanaba:** "Escanaba golfers who have driven past the signs on US-2 and never stopped are the ones who are most surprised when they finally play." — excellent.
- **Gladstone:** "Gladstone sits on Little Bay de Noc. Nahma sits on Big Bay de Noc, the wider bay to the west." — smart geographic contrast.
- **Garden:** The Fayette Historic State Park connection is genuine local knowledge and earns the recommendation.
- **Rapid River:** "The course has been here since 1922 waiting for you to find it." — works.

**Flags:**

**Manistique page:** "Golfers coming through for a UP trip often end up booking the same round the next morning before they leave." — Nahma doesn't take bookings. This line reads as fabricated social proof and should be cut. It's also slightly incoherent (booking a same-day walk-in course?).

**All five pages:** The five "About the Course" fact cards (9 Holes, Est. 1922, $18, Big Bay de Noc, Public) are structurally fine as SEO anchors. But the card text varies inconsistently across pages. Escanaba says "$18" while Manistique says "9 holes for $18. 18 holes for $28." Pick one version and standardize.

---

### Blog (`/blog/`)

One post live, honest placeholder for more. No copy issues with what's here. "dispatches from the shore of Big Bay de Noc" is a good description line.
